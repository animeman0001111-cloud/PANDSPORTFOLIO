Chapter 5

Orthogonality

Dot Products, Orthogonal Vectors, Projections, Gram-Schmidt Process, QR Decomposition, Least Squares and Orthogonal Matrices

Introduction to Orthogonality

Orthogonality is one of the most important concepts in Linear Algebra. It is used in Computer Graphics, Machine Learning, Robotics, Signal Processing, Computer Vision and Artificial Intelligence.

Orthogonality describes vectors that are perpendicular to each other. It helps in measuring similarity, removing redundancy and simplifying computations.

Orthogonal vectors satisfy: u · v = 0

Orthogonal systems make calculations easier because vectors become independent from each other.

Dot Product

The dot product (inner product) of two vectors measures how much they point in the same direction.

u · v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ + ... + uₙvₙ u · v = ‖u‖ ‖v‖ cos θ
  • If u · v = 0, vectors are orthogonal (90°)
  • If u · v > 0, the angle between them is acute
  • If u · v < 0, the angle between them is obtuse

Projections

The projection of vector b onto vector a is the component of b that lies along a. It is the closest point on the line through a to the tip of b.

proj_a(b) = (a · b / a · a) × a Scalar projection: comp_a(b) = (a · b) / ‖a‖

Projections are used in least squares fitting, decomposing forces in physics, and feature extraction in machine learning.

Gram-Schmidt Process

The Gram-Schmidt process converts any set of linearly independent vectors into an orthonormal basis — vectors that are mutually perpendicular and each have unit length.

1

Take v₁ — normalise it: e₁ = v₁ / ‖v₁‖

2

Subtract projection of v₂ onto e₁, then normalise: e₂ = (v₂ − proj) / ‖v₂ − proj‖

3

Repeat for each subsequent vector, subtracting all previous projections.

QR Decomposition

QR Decomposition factors a matrix A into an orthogonal matrix Q and an upper triangular matrix R:

A = Q × R Q: orthogonal (QᵀQ = I) R: upper triangular

QR decomposition is produced by the Gram-Schmidt process. It is used for solving least squares problems, computing eigenvalues, and numerical stability in linear system solvers.

Least Squares

When Ax = b has no exact solution (overdetermined system), we find the best approximate solution x̂ that minimises ‖Ax − b‖².

Normal equations: AᵀAx̂ = Aᵀb Solution: x̂ = (AᵀA)⁻¹ Aᵀb

Application: Linear regression in machine learning uses least squares to find the best-fit line through a cloud of data points, minimising the sum of squared residuals.
