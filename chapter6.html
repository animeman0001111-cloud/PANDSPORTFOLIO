Chapter 6

Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

Invariant Directions, Matrix Transformations, Diagonalization, PageRank, Markov Chains, Network Analysis and Applications in Computer Science and Engineering

Introduction

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are one of the most important concepts in Linear Algebra. They help us understand how matrices transform vectors and identify directions that remain unchanged during transformations.

A · v = λ · v A = matrix v = eigenvector (direction unchanged) λ = eigenvalue (scaling factor)

When a matrix A is applied to its eigenvector v, the result is simply v scaled by the eigenvalue λ — the direction does not change, only the magnitude.

Finding Eigenvalues

Eigenvalues are found by solving the characteristic equation. This is obtained by requiring that the transformation (A − λI) collapses some non-zero vector to zero:

1

Form the characteristic equation: det(A − λI) = 0

2

Expand the determinant to get the characteristic polynomial in λ

3

Solve the polynomial — each root is an eigenvalue λ

4

Substitute each λ back into (A − λI)v = 0 to find corresponding eigenvectors

Diagonalization

A matrix A is diagonalizable if it can be written as:

A = P D P⁻¹ P = matrix of eigenvectors (columns) D = diagonal matrix of eigenvalues

Diagonalization simplifies repeated matrix powers: A^n = P D^n P⁻¹, and since D is diagonal, D^n just raises each diagonal entry to the nth power. This is extremely useful for computing Markov chain steady states.

Google PageRank Algorithm

PageRank models the web as a directed graph. The link structure is encoded as a matrix M where each entry represents the probability of moving from one page to another. The ranking of every page is the eigenvector corresponding to eigenvalue λ = 1 of this transition matrix — the steady-state vector that the system converges to after many random walks.

M · r = r (r is the rank vector, λ = 1)

Markov Chains

A Markov chain describes a system that transitions between states with fixed probabilities. The state evolves as:

xₙ₊₁ = A · xₙ → xₙ = Aⁿ · x₀

The long-run (steady-state) distribution is the eigenvector of A with eigenvalue 1. Diagonalization makes computing Aⁿ efficient even for large n.

Applications

🤖 Artificial Intelligence

PCA uses eigenvectors to find the principal components that explain data variance.

🌐 Network Analysis

Centrality measures in social networks are computed from the dominant eigenvector.

🎮 Computer Graphics

Inertia tensors in 3D physics engines are diagonalized to find principal axes of rotation.

📡 Signal Processing

Fourier and wavelet transforms decompose signals into eigenfunctions of linear operators.

🦾 Robotics

Vibration analysis of robot structures uses eigenvalues to predict resonant frequencies.

🧬 Machine Learning

Spectral clustering and dimensionality reduction rely on matrix eigenvectors.
